Steps for Utility Planning
1

Assess Current Infrastructure

Evaluate your existing electrical capacity before planning any installation. Use the NES Electric Service Guidelines Table 1 for service ratings.

  • Check main service panel capacity (amps rating)
  • Identify distance from panel to charging location
  • Determine if existing transformer can handle additional load
  • Review any existing load studies or electrical plans
2

Contact NES Energy Services

Reach out to NES for an initial assessment of your electrical infrastructure. Contact them at least 30 days in advance for residential or 60 days for commercial projects.

  • Call: (615) 747-3775
  • Email: [email protected]
  • Discuss transformer capacity, service lines, and load impacts
  • Get cost estimates for any required upgrades (estimates valid 30 days)
3

Submit Application & Site Plan

If NES involvement is required, submit formal application per Site Plan Requirements.

  • Complete NES application for new/upgraded service
  • Provide civil site plan showing charger locations
  • Include load calculations and equipment specifications
  • Pay any required deposit or CIAC fees
4

Obtain Permits from Metro Codes

Secure all required building and electrical permits per Metro Code Title 16.

  • Electrical permit: $75 minimum + per-circuit fees
  • Service release permit if upgrading service
  • Building permit if structural work required
  • Submit plans for review (may take 2-4 weeks)
5

Installation & Inspections

Complete installation following NES guidelines (Tables 5 & 9) and schedule inspections.

  • Trenching: 42" for residential, 48" for commercial
  • Use #67 gravel and meet NES backfill requirements
  • Request NES ditch inspection before backfill (if underground)
  • Schedule Metro Codes electrical inspection
6

NES Service Release & Energization

Final steps per Guidelines ¬ß6.2 and Metro Code ¬ß16.04.100.

  • NES requires electrical release from Metro Codes
  • NES installs/swaps meter after inspection passes
  • Service is energized and chargers activated
  • Set up billing account (EVC rate if eligible)

When is NES Involvement Required?

The need for NES involvement depends on whether your installation requires changes to the utility service or meter. Here's a quick decision guide:

üî¥ NES IS Required

  • New or Upgraded Service: Upgrading from 200A to 400A or higher for EV chargers
  • New Transformer: Installing DC Fast Chargers (50kW+) or multiple chargers requiring larger transformer
  • New Metered Account: Adding a separate meter for EV charging (public or pay-per-use)
  • Distribution Changes: Relocating overhead lines or burying cables for EV canopy
  • CT Metering: Any service above 200A requiring current transformers
  • Solar/Battery Export: Systems that can export power back to the grid

üü¢ NES NOT Required

  • Small Addition: Adding a Level 2 (30-40A) charger to existing 200A panel with spare capacity
  • Subpanel Only: Adding a subpanel for EV charging without upsizing main service
  • Same-Rating Replacement: Swapping meter base with same amperage (no wire changes)
  • Off-Grid Backup: Installing an open-transition generator that never parallels with NES
  • Breaker Replacement: Replacing breakers in existing panel

Release Types

‚ö° Electric Release Required When:

  • Installing a new service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service or building new service
  • Service has been de-energized over one year

üìù Change Meter Order Required When:

  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service
  • Replacing an existing service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Whenever an Electrical Release is required

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Multifamily is Commercial

Under NES Electric Service Guidelines Chapter III, multifamily buildings (condos, apartments) are classified as commercial service, even if residents are using chargers for personal vehicles. Three-phase service is also considered commercial. This affects rates, metering requirements, and service classifications.

üìù Homeowner Permits (¬ß16.20.040)

Nashville allows homeowners to obtain permits for electrical work on their principal dwelling, but only if: (1) the work is within average DIY capability, (2) the homeowner does the work themselves without help from contractors, (3) only one electrical permit is issued within a 12-month period, and (4) it is not for commercial purposes.

Frequently Asked Questions
What permits are needed for EV charger installation? +

At minimum, you need an electrical permit ($75 minimum). If you're upgrading your service, you'll also need a service release permit ($75 residential, $102 commercial). Building permits may be required if structural work is needed.

What is a Contribution in Aid of Construction (CIAC)? +

CIAC is a fee charged when NES infrastructure upgrades (transformers, conductors, etc.) are required per NES Electric Service Policies ¬ß1.3. NES provides cost estimates during the engineering phase. This fee covers the portion of infrastructure costs that exceed NES's standard allowable investment.

How long does the NES approval process take? +

Applications should be submitted 30 days in advance for residential or 60 days for commercial. Simple service upgrades may take 2-4 weeks after approval. Projects requiring new transformers can take 6-18 months due to supply chain delays. Cost estimates are valid for 30 days.

Can I use the EVC rate for my condo charging station? +

The EVC rate is available for installations with demand between 50 kW and 5,000 kW. Most single Level 2 chargers won't qualify, but larger installations with multiple chargers or DC fast chargers may be eligible. Contact NES to discuss rate options.

What is a service release vs. a change meter order? +

Service Release: Required for NEW services or reconnecting de-energized services (over 1 year). Involves full NES engineering and Codes inspection per Guidelines ¬ß6.2.

Change Meter Order: Required when upgrading existing service (panel size change, conductor replacement, relocation). Also requires Codes release per Metro Code ¬ß16.04.100.

What about supply chain delays? +

NES is currently experiencing significant supply chain delays for transformers, poles, and meters. Plan for 12-18 month lead times on major infrastructure. NES has approximately $80M in backorders arriving incrementally. Consider the "Bring Your Own Transformer" policy for DC Fast Charger installations to potentially reduce delays.
